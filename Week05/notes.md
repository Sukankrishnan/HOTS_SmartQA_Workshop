**Topic: The Geometry Of Understanding**

You get clarity when you take notes and revisit it. Clarity often begins with fragments. When you don't structure it, it may lose the essence of the core idea of understanding. 

Before testing a piece of software, we gather many insights. For instance, the environment we use, how data flows through the system, which URL calls which API, who the users are, and what responses we expect. Each of these observations gives clarity about what to test.

These are fragmented. When you don't know how to test the software with which test environment, which endpoints hits the server and you get the response, you are not structured your tests. Structure may not be a test plan document, test strategy, or test report. But it could be a mental architecture where the sequence of elements falls into one place to give a picture.
 
    Without organising our thoughts, testing feels like scattered pieces.
    Without understanding the details, you perceive only a rough outline of the system. 
    Structure without clarity can turn into empty abstraction.
 
Testing becomes powerful when scattered observations turn into a structured understanding of the system.
